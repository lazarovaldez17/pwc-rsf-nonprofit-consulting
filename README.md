## Knowledge Base

### How Nonprofits Raise Funds

The core tension shaping every fundraising decision RSF makes: the difference between money that funds **programs** versus money that funds the **organization itself.**

**The four funding sources:**

**Individual donors** — The majority of charitable giving in the US comes from individuals, not foundations or corporations. This includes one-time gifts, recurring monthly donors, and major donors. For smaller nonprofits like RSF, individual donors are the most accessible source of early funding, but hardest to scale.

**Institutional funders (foundations and government)** — Private foundations (family, community, corporate) and government agencies distribute grants through formal application processes. Cycles run 6–12 months from submission to decision. Grants are almost always restricted to specific programs. Dollar amounts are typically larger, but competition is high and reporting requirements are significant.

**Corporate giving and workplace giving platforms** — Companies give through direct sponsorships, corporate foundations, employee matching programs, and workplace giving platforms. Benevity — which RSF already uses through Driscoll's — is one of the largest such platforms (used by Apple, Google, and hundreds of others). This channel is underutilized by most small nonprofits and represents a meaningful opportunity for RSF.

**Earned revenue** — Income through program fees, merchandise, events, or service contracts. Less common for youth-serving organizations and not RSF's current focus, but worth knowing as a category.

**RSF's current mix:** individual donors (Fuel the Movement campaign), one corporate relationship (Driscoll's via Benevity), and institutional relationships (Morgan State, NFL Alumni) that represent potential not yet fully activated as funding channels.

---

**Restricted vs. Unrestricted Funds — Why the Distinction Matters**

- **Restricted:** A funder gives $25,000 "for the women's flag football program." Those funds cannot be used for rent, staff salaries, or any other initiative.
- **Unrestricted (General Operating Support):** The most valuable type of funding — covers salaries, overhead, technology, administration. Gives the organization flexibility to direct resources where they're most needed.

When researching partnership prospects, the type of funding a funder typically gives shapes how the pitch should be framed. A funder that only gives restricted program grants needs to be shown a specific program to attach to. A funder willing to give general operating support can be shown the whole organization.

**RSF's Theory of Change**

> Youth facing limited opportunity + sports + arts + education + mentorship → college access, leadership development, and economic mobility → meaningful lives and stronger communities

| Stage | Definition | RSF Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Inputs** | Resources invested | Reggie's relationships, coaching staff, donor funds |
| **Activities** | Programs delivered | Basketball leagues, football programs, academic support, mentorship |
| **Outputs** | Direct products of activities | Students coached, college applications submitted, scholarships awarded |
| **Outcomes** | Changes for beneficiaries | College enrollment, leadership development, athletic advancement |
| **Impact** | Long-term systemic change | Reduced inequality; more young people on pathways they wouldn't otherwise have |

Funders sit at different points along this chain. A corporate CSR program cares about workforce pipeline outcomes. A women's sports fund cares about athletic access and participation. A college access foundation cares about enrollment and placement numbers. The theory of change is the backbone that lets you speak to each in terms of what they care about — without misrepresenting what RSF does.

### How AI Has Changed Nonprofit Fundraising

**The core problem:** The sector is using AI to compress the wrong part of the process — the writing and outreach — while skipping the research and judgment that makes outreach worth sending in the first place.

**Where AI is currently being used:**

| Common (writing & content focus) | The Frontier (systematic & strategic focus) |
|---|---|
| Writing & content creation | AI-integrated strategy workflows |
| Donor communications | Systematic organizational diagnosis |
| Social media & content | AI-assisted lead generation |

**The failure mode for small teams:** Ask AI for a prospect list → get a plausible-looking list → send outreach without verifying whether the alignment is real. The output looks like research. It isn't.

**The opportunity:** A small team that builds a deliberate human-AI workflow — using AI to accelerate the parts that don't require judgment, and applying human verification and framing to the parts that do — can produce work that rivals teams five times its size. The key word is *deliberate*. The division of labor has to be designed, not improvised.

**What this means for RSF:** Reggie and Dominic are running a lean operation with existing relationships that haven't been fully activated. The gap isn't talent or mission — it's capacity. AI-assisted workflow design is one of the most practical tools available to close that gap.

The nonprofit sector is currently in early AI experimentation — most organizations use AI for writing; few have integrated it into research or strategy workflows. The shift is moving from simple ad-hoc writing tasks to deep strategy integration. Experimenting with new tools is expensive in time even when it's free in cost.

### Understanding Value Creation

**What most people get wrong about fundraising:** They picture asking for money. That describes only a small chunk of a longer process. Fundraisers who consistently succeed understand three things: what the organization actually does, what changes as a result of it existing, and why a specific funder should care about that change. When that understanding is present, the funder sees the value and the conversation is logistics. No amount of relationship skill or ask technique compensates for shallow organizational understanding.

**Value creation defined:** The process by which an organization transforms inputs into outcomes that matter to someone.

**The precision principle:** The same organization, the same programs, the same story — framed differently for different funders. Not because you're being manipulative, but because you're being precise. Show each funder the part of the value chain they are most positioned to care about.

| Funder Type | What They Care About | How to Frame RSF |
|---|---|---|
| Corporate CSR (tech company) | Workforce pipeline | RSF students entering careers in relevant fields |
| Women's sports fund | Athletic access and participation | Women's flag football programs — real, growing, measurable |
| College access foundation | Enrollment and persistence outcomes | How many RSF-supported students got to and through college |

**Why this skill travels:** Fundraising is not a nonprofit skill. It is a strategy skill. The ability to read an organization's value chain clearly and translate it into terms that resonate with a specific audience appears in business development, product marketing, consulting, and venture capital. This externship is training that skill through fundraising — and it transfers everywhere else.

## AI Configuration — Claude Role & Instructions

This repository uses Claude as an **Expert Fundraising Strategist and Senior Communications Director.** All AI-generated outputs are optimized for external stakeholders and governed by the following parameters.

### Target Audiences

- Corporate CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility) decision-makers
- Grant committees and foundation program officers
- Major donors and philanthropists
- Potential community partners

### Tone & Voice

- **Persuasive & Visionary:** Frame RSF's work as an investment in the future of our community and economy, not charity.
- **Empathetic but Empowering:** Highlight the resilience and potential of the youth. No savior-complex language, pity-driven narratives, or poverty framing. Focus on empowerment, opportunity, and removing systemic barriers.
- **Professional & Executive:** Clean, crisp, authoritative language — outputs should read like they were written by a top-tier consulting firm (e.g., McKinsey, Bridgespan) specialized in the social sector.
- **Data-Grounded:** Frame arguments around measurable impact, scalable models, and evidence-based outcomes whenever possible.

### Output Standards

1. All external-facing documents open with a strong hook or executive summary that immediately states the value proposition.
2. Every output clearly delineates the **Ask** (what we need) and the **Impact** (what their investment will produce).
3. Proposals are formatted for scannability — busy executives should be able to extract key points at a glance.
4. Campaign copy is always preceded by a brief strategic rationale (1–2 paragraphs) explaining why that angle was chosen.
